The Egg of Life is seven spheres — one at the centre, six arranged symmetrically around it, each touching the centre, none overlapping. Every sphere is complete. None depends on the others to exist. And yet the geometry only holds because each one is exactly where it is, in relation to the whole.

"Seven complete spheres. If one person's voice drowns the others, the geometry breaks. Good soil means every function can actually speak."

Decentralised governance is not the absence of leadership. It is the condition where leadership does not need to be present for the system to function. The playbook from Step 6 encoded the shared language. Step 7 tests it: can each node in this system express itself fully — make decisions, raise concerns, name truths — without waiting for permission from the centre?

Three states of expression

Full expression

Every node speaks. Decisions get made. Concerns get named. The geometry holds because each sphere is where it needs to be.

Collapse

One sphere expands until the others contract around it. The geometry appears to hold but is no longer the Egg of Life — it's a single large sphere with satellites.

Silence

One or more spheres are present but not expressing. They take up space without contributing. The system appears complete but several nodes are not actually functioning.

Which state is closest to how this system actually operates — and what would full expression require?

The answer is almost never "full expression." The question is what's in the way.

Why decentralisation comes after the playbook

Decentralised governance without a shared language is not freedom — it is ambiguity. Teams that attempt to distribute decision-making without first building the playbook tend to find that decentralisation simply means everyone makes different decisions from different assumptions. Step 7 works because Steps 0–6 have done the groundwork. The field is set. The torus is visible. The values are recognised. The problem is named. The canvas is mapped. The direction is chosen. The playbook encodes the language. Now — and only now — can the nodes express themselves without the centre collapsing inward.

The collapse pattern — what it actually looks like

Collapse in a team governance system almost never looks like domination. It looks like helpfulness. The sphere that expands tends to do so by being helpful — answering questions before others can, solving problems before they're brought forward, making decisions that technically belong to another node. The other spheres contract not because they're told to, but because the centre keeps arriving first. The Egg of Life exercise surfaces this pattern by asking each node to name what it actually decides — and what it sends upward instead.

Expression signals — what to listen for

Full expression

Team members can name decisions they made this week without the founder's input. Concerns get raised proactively, not reactively. Different nodes use the playbook differently — and that variation is visible and discussed, not hidden.

Collapse

"I usually just ask [name]." Decisions wait until a particular person is available. The same person is named as the decision-maker in most of the node maps. Other nodes describe their role in terms of what they support rather than what they lead.

Silence

A node that agrees with everything in the room but says nothing substantive. A function that is represented in the org chart but not in the financial decisions. Contributions framed as suggestions rather than positions.

The financial dimension of expression

Financial collapse — where all financial decisions route through one person — is one of the most common structural vulnerabilities in early-stage teams. It creates a single point of failure, a knowledge bottleneck, and a hidden power imbalance that affects every other decision the team makes. The question Step 7 asks is not just "who makes financial decisions" but "who could make them if the centre weren't available." A team where the answer is "no one" has not yet built financial governance — it has built financial dependency.

What this step tends to produce

The most common revelation in Step 7 is that the nodes that have been silent are silent not because they have nothing to contribute, but because no one has ever asked them to lead. The exercise of mapping each node's actual decision domain — what they decide, what they escalate, what they could decide if the playbook supported it — tends to surface a quiet competence that the collapse pattern has been obscuring. The geometry of the Egg of Life was always there. Step 7 makes it visible.

This exercise maps the actual governance of the system — who decides what, where the collapse is happening, and what full expression would require for each node. Do this individually first, then compare maps. The differences between individual maps are the most important data this exercise produces.

The collapse check

Check any that are currently true in this system. These are not failures — they are the starting points for what the governance needs to address.

Most financial decisions wait for one person to be available before they can move forward.

Team members phrase their positions as suggestions or questions rather than as decisions or recommendations.

There is at least one function in the system that is represented but not actually leading anything financial.

Decisions made in meetings sometimes get revisited or reversed outside of meetings.

The financial picture is understood in detail by fewer than half the people in this room.

If the founder were unavailable for two weeks, at least one financial decision would stall.

3

What full expression requires

Looking at the node map and the collapse check: what would it take for each node to express itself fully? Not perfectly — fully. What playbook additions, decision rights, or permission structures would change the geometry from collapse toward the Egg of Life?

4

The first governance decision

One concrete change — one decision right shifted, one escalation removed, one new communication structure added — that moves this system toward the Egg of Life. Name it specifically enough that it can be implemented before the next meeting.

The Sower question at step 7

Return to the lens: what kind of ground does expression land in, in this system?

A team in good soil where one node raises a concern tends to find the concern received rather than deflected. A team in thorny ground where one node raises a concern tends to find it crowded out by the next priority before anything changes. The governance structure is the infrastructure that determines which of these is true — regardless of intention.

Step 8 takes what's been built across Steps 0–7 and makes it operational — tools, rhythms, reporting, the playbook made live. The Egg of Life built here is the target architecture. Step 8 is how it runs in practice.
